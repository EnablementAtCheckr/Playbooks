Your Role

Enterprise Account Executive Overview

What Enterprise AE means at Checkr, who you sell to, and what you own.

The Enterprise Sales role at Checkr focuses on driving revenue with large, complex customers — typically organizations with 5,000+ employees or $450M+ in annual revenue. Deal sizes generally range from $100K to over $1M, with sales cycles averaging 3–9 months depending on deal complexity.

While Commercial AEs emphasize velocity, Enterprise AEs emphasize depth — building alignment across HR, Talent, and IT, crafting ROI-driven business cases, and ensuring a smooth handoff from bookings to revenue once customers begin running checks. You'll navigate multi-stakeholder sales cycles, often partnering with Solution Consultants, Legal, and Security.

Self-Generated Pipeline

Outbound Motion

15% of your pipeline — ~100–150 target accounts, 3–5 stakeholders per account, multi-threaded outreach.

Enterprise AEs are expected to generate approximately 15% of their pipeline through self-sourced outbound. You'll be assigned a focused list of ~100–150 target accounts that meet the Enterprise ICP (5,000+ employees, $450M+ annual revenue), viewable in SFDC under your manager's hierarchy.

Outreach should be multi-threaded and strategic — leveraging phone, email, and LinkedIn to build alignment and uncover business pain. All activity goes through Outreach. Block at least one hour daily for focused outbound prospecting. Target a minimum of 1–2 new outbound SQOs per month.

Multi-Threading Strategy

Enterprise Stakeholder Map

Multi-thread from day one. Enterprise deals are lost when a single contact goes dark.

In enterprise deals you need relationships at multiple levels. Your champion gets you in — but your champion alone won't close the deal. Map and engage every stakeholder from the first discovery call onward.

Economic Buyer
CHRO / VP People
Cares about: Employer brand, compliance risk, board-level metrics, total cost of workforce operations. Not in the weeds of screening ops.
Entry point: Go through HR Ops champion first. Never cold-approach the CHRO — reach them with a warm intro and a clear business case.
Key Influencer
VP / Director of TA
Cares about: Time-to-fill, candidate conversion rates, ATS integration reliability, screening drop-off at the offer stage.
Entry point: Lead with screening turnaround pain and candidate experience data. Can become a champion if their pain is acute.
Best Champion
HR Operations / People Ops
Cares about: Day-to-day screening workflow, vendor headaches, turnaround SLAs, error rates, adjudication complexity.
Entry point: Your most likely champion. Engage early and deep. Know their workflow cold. Coach them to sell Checkr internally when you're not in the room.
Gatekeeper / Required
Legal / Compliance
Cares about: FCRA compliance, adverse action, ban-the-box, data privacy, contract terms and MSA redlines.
Entry point: Engage through your champion. Have Raul Moreno (VP Legal) available for complex legal questions. Don't let legal become a blocker — pre-empt objections.
Late-Stage Required
Procurement
Cares about: Total cost of ownership, contract terms, vendor consolidation, compliance with vendor management policies.
Entry point: Late-stage only. Your champion and EB should brief Procurement before you engage. Come with a clear business case and TCO analysis.
Technical Evaluator
IT / HRIS
Cares about: ATS integration complexity, data security, API reliability, InfoSec compliance, SSO and directory requirements.
Entry point: Needed for security review. Engage your SE early so they can build a technical relationship in parallel with your business relationship.

Champion Development

In enterprise deals, your champion is everything. Without a strong internal advocate who actively sells Checkr when you're not in the room, you will lose deals you should win.

Building a Strong Champion
  • Identify your champion early — typically HR Ops or VP of TA who has direct pain
  • Coach them on how to sell internally — give them the business case language
  • Test them: "If I'm not in the room, what do you say when someone asks why Checkr?"
  • Multi-thread above your champion — their departure should not kill a deal
  • Share proof points, case studies, and ROI data they can use in internal conversations
Business Case Creation
  • Quantify the Before Scenario with the prospect's own numbers — turnaround time, drop-off rate, compliance incidents
  • Build the ROI case in their language: what does fixing this mean in dollars?
  • Use the Meeting Prep doc template (see Confluence) before every multi-stakeholder call
  • Create an SA (Success Agreement) for complex deals — align on what success looks like before close
  • Executive Engagement Playbook in Confluence — use it for C-suite meetings

Solution Engineering

SE Partnership & Rules of Engagement

SEs are deal accelerators — not gatekeepers. You are the deal quarterback. Use SEs strategically.

Solution Engineers add credibility on technical and product depth questions that you can't answer alone. Brief them well, use them at the right moments, and debrief after every engagement.

ScenarioInvolve SE?How
Prospect asks detailed technical integration questions (ATS, API, data security)YesBrief SE before the call. They handle technical depth while you own the relationship and the room.
Deal is >$100K moving into Establish RequirementsYesProactively bring SE in. Complex deals need technical alignment early, not as a last-minute add.
Security review or InfoSec questionnaire requiredYesSE leads the security review process. Introduce them to the prospect's IT/security contact directly.
Demo for a multi-stakeholder group including IT or HRISYesSE co-presents technical aspects. You present the business case and run the room.
Simple discovery call with an HR Ops buyerTypically noRun discovery solo. Bring SE in when technical requirements surface in the conversation.
Pricing call with HR buyer onlyTypically noYou own pricing conversations. SE is not needed unless technical contract terms are being negotiated.
🏈
You Are the Deal Quarterback
How to brief your SE effectively
Before any SE involvement, share: (1) deal context and where you are in the process, (2) what the prospect cares most about, (3) what technical questions have come up, and (4) what outcome you need from the SE engagement. A well-briefed SE closes deals. An under-briefed SE creates confusion.

Post-Close

Revenue Management

Bookings → Revenue is your responsibility. You own it until Implementation hands off to CS.

As an Enterprise AE, you don't just close deals — you ensure your closed deals convert to active, revenue-generating customers. Enterprise deals that don't ramp are a direct hit to your revenue number.

Post-Close Activities — Complete on Every Deal

Required Post-Close Actions
  • PennDOT / Utah DOT — State-specific compliance requirements for MVR checks
  • Credit Check configuration — FCRA-compliant setup if applicable to the deal
  • ESS Jira Ticket — Submit the customer setup ticket to Implementation
  • Expected ramp timeline — Provide Implementation with a realistic estimate of when the customer will run first checks
Handoff Process
  • Schedule kickoff call between customer, Implementation Specialist, and yourself
  • Provide Implementation with a full deal summary: products, volumes, ATS integration, key contacts
  • Stay involved through the first 4 weeks post-kickoff — you're the business-side escalation point
  • After 4 weeks: Implementation hands off to CSM (if ACV >$10K) or Tech Touch (if ACV <$10K)
  • Reference the B2R Playbook in Confluence for escalation procedures

When ROE is Broken
